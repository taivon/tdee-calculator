What is TDEE?

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is the number of calories your body burns in a typical day. It combines the energy needed to keep you alive at rest with the energy you spend moving, exercising, and digesting food. Knowing your TDEE is the foundation for setting calorie targets—whether you want to lose fat, maintain your weight, or build muscle.

The largest piece of TDEE is your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)—the calories burned to maintain basic functions like breathing, circulation, and cell repair while you are at complete rest. BMR depends on your sex, age, height, and weight. Established formulas such as Mifflin-St Jeor estimate BMR from these inputs; if you know your body fat percentage, lean-mass formulas like Katch-McArdle can be more accurate for your composition.

Daily activity adds the next layer. A desk job with little exercise burns far fewer calories than a physically active lifestyle or regular training program. Activity multipliers scale your BMR upward to reflect how often and how hard you move—from sedentary through very active levels.

A smaller but real component is the Thermic Effect of Food (TEF)—energy used to digest, absorb, and process what you eat. Protein has a higher TEF than carbohydrates or fat, which is one reason balanced macro splits matter alongside total calories.

Put together: TDEE ≈ BMR × activity level, with TEF included in practical estimates. Our calculator uses your inputs to estimate BMR, apply your activity level, and suggest calorie targets for cutting, maintaining, or bulking—plus a starting macro split to support your goal.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is TDEE and how is it calculated?

TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) is the total calories you burn per day. We estimate your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) from your sex, age, height, and weight, then multiply by an activity factor that reflects how active you are. The result is your estimated daily calorie needs before any intentional deficit or surplus.

How accurate is this calculator?

Formula-based estimates are useful starting points, but individual metabolism varies with genetics, hormones, sleep, stress, and medical conditions. Treat the number as an educated estimate—track your weight and energy over two to four weeks and adjust calories up or down based on real-world results.

Which formula should I use?

Mifflin-St Jeor is the default and works well for most people. If you know your body fat percentage, Katch-McArdle (based on lean body mass) is often more accurate. The advanced panel lets you compare multiple formulas side by side so you can see how estimates differ for your stats.

How do I pick the right activity level?

Choose the level that best describes your typical week—not your most intense day. Sedentary fits a desk job with little exercise; Light covers one to three light sessions per week; Moderate fits three to five moderate workouts; Active and Very Active are for frequent hard training or physically demanding jobs. When in doubt, start conservative and adjust calories based on progress.

How many calories should I eat to lose weight?

A common approach is eating below your TDEE by about 500 calories per day, which targets roughly one pound of fat loss per week for many people. Smaller deficits (250 calories) are gentler and easier to sustain. Avoid very large deficits without medical supervision—they can cost muscle, energy, and long-term adherence.

What are macros and why do they matter?

Macronutrients—protein, carbohydrates, and fat—are the calorie-containing nutrients in food. Protein supports muscle repair and satiety; carbs fuel training and daily activity; fats support hormones and nutrient absorption. Total calories drive weight change, but macro balance helps you feel better, perform in the gym, and preserve lean mass while dieting.
